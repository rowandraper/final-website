2021: Stanton Territorial Hospital, Yellowknife, North-West Territories


During an operation to remove an Inuk woman's right fallopian tube and possibly her right ovary, Dr. Andrew Kotaska additionally removed an Inuk woman's left fallopian tube and left ovary without her consent, leaving her infertile.

Sources:
(2024) Senate of Canada - Hon. Yvonne Boyer
(2022) CBC News - Julia Peterson
(2022) CBC News - Avery Zingel

In order to address a painful cyst on her right ovary, a 37-year-old woman from Tuktoyaktuk, NWT consented to the removal of her right fallopian tube and, if necessary, her right ovary (marginally reducing her chances of concieving). The operation was conducted in November of 2021, during which Dr. Kotaska brazenly stated out loud, "Let's see if I can find a reason to take the left tube", ignoring the warnings from his colleagues, before removing both it and her left ovary, leaving the woman infertile.

A civil suit was launched in April 2021 against Kotaska and the North-west Territories Health and Social Services Authority for $6.5 million. They both filed statements of defence, and Andrew Kotaska denied sterilizing her without consent. A year later, he publicly apologized. In his defence, he stated that his medical student heard the Inuk patient say she did not want any more children, seemingly implying that if an organ was not being used, it shouldn’t be a problem if he removed it — as if she didn’t need it anyway.

1998-1999: Vernon Jubille Hospital, Vernon, British Columbia


A member of the Wet'suwet'en nation was physically forced into an unconscious state via anesthethia by nurses and doctors, and had an abortion performed on her without her consent.

Sources:
(2023) Supreme Court of British Columbia


The woman was a teenager and pregnant with her first child, scheduled to undergo an abortion at Vernon Jubilee Hospital. At the time, she felt pressured to undergo the abortion at the behest of her boyfriend, his mother, and the hospital's attending doctor. She was not given information about the procedure, and was not given information on aftercare, side effects or counselling.

Immediately before the abortion procedure began, the woman advised the medical professionals present that she did not want to have the abortion; she told them that she had changed her mind and did not want to proceed with it. Her advisement, and pleas to not go ahead with the abortion, went unheeded by the nurses and doctors; one of these medical professionals said to the woman, "it's too late" and "you already signed the papers". Still crying and repeating "no", the woman was then held down by the medical staff and had a mask put on her face, rendering her unconscious. When she woke up, her baby was gone.

September 1983: Campbell River Hospital, Campbell River, British-Columbia


Moments before the scheduled delivery of her second child by cesarian section, a First Nations woman was presented with paperwork authorizing a "tubal ligation". She signed the papers not knowing what she was signing, and having recieved no prior explanation or medical advice about sterilization. There was no valid medical reason for the procedure.

Sources:
(2023) Supreme Court of British Columbia

Additionally, the woman was not informed of the effects or the permanence of the procedure, and was not given information on aftercare, side effects or counselling. She was not provided with options for alternative forms of birth control. Nor was her husband given any information or advice concerning the procedure.

Following the delivery of her child, several weeks passed before the woman began to realize and remember what had happened. She suffered from severe post-partum depression. When she and her husband later wished to have another child, they were unable to do so.

2023: Forced Sterilization and Abortion Class Action Lawsuit


This lawsuit, filed jointly by Cooper Regal in Alberta and Murphy Battista in B.C. seeks compensation for "sexism, discrimination and cultural genocide" due to the provincial government's nearly half-a-century policy of coercing Indigenous women into sterilizations. This lawsuit pertains to both the incident in Vernon and Campbell River, British-Columbia.

Sources:
(2023) Supreme Court of British Columbia
(2023) Murphy Battista Law Firm

Murphy Battista LLP and Cooper Regal LLP have filed a proposed class action against His Majesty the King in the Right of the Province of British Columbia (the “Province”) for wilfully and knowingly failing to address the practice of coerced sterilization and abortion of Indigenous women in British Columbia that occurred between August 1, 1974 and the present.

The Claim alleges that the Province was complicit in creating an atmosphere of institutional and systemic racism in provincially-funded and regulated hospitals throughout British Columbia where coerced sterilization and abortion was implicitly and explicitly condoned, encouraged, authorized and performed by the Province and/or its agents, servants or employees.

As a result of the Province’s acts and omissions, the Claim alleges that Indigenous women were subjected to coerced sterilization and/or coerced abortion in British Columbia and have suffered physical, emotional, spiritual, mental and psychological harm. The practice of coerced sterilization and abortion has deprived Indigenous women of fundamental choices guaranteed to all Canadians with respect to reproductive capacity.

The Claim alleges that the practice of coerced sterilization and abortion has had a traumatic and destructive effect on the health, family relationships and culture of Indigenous women and Indigenous communities in British Columbia. Further, the practice was, and remains a form of sexism and genocide – a practice directed at eradicating Indigenous people and their cultures.

The plaintiffs bring the Claim on their own behalf, and on behalf of class members, to seek justice and compensation for the harms suffered as a result of the Province’s breach of Indigenous women’s trust, dignity, bodily autonomy, and reproductive and cultural rights.

December 2018: Unknown Location

Moments before the scheduled delivery of her third child by cesarian section, the anaesthesiologist abruptly and agressively directed a Nakota woman to sign a consent form for the C-section, which included a tubal ligation. The woman believed she had no choice but to sign.

Sources:
(2019) Canada House of Commons

The 30-year-old Nakota woman did not recall prior conversations regarding a tubal ligation, and did not want one. SHe wished to have more children.

Following her Caesaerian section, the woman was sterilized. She was devastated and immediately asked a nurse whether the operation was reversible.

2010: Saskatoon Royal University Hospital, SK


Immediately after the birth of her seventh child, a hospital social worker pressured an Cree Métis woman to have her tubes tied, telling her "seven children was enough" and that she couldn't leave until she had a tubal ligation. That night, nurses continued to pressure her to consent to the procedure.
The following morning, a nurse told the woman that an operating room was being prepared for her procedure. The same social worker and a doctor then pressured her again to sign the consent form. The victim stated: "I was tired and frustrated and I signed it against my will to get them to leave me alone".
Additionally, she went into the procedure thinking it would be reversible, as she was told that her Fallopian tubes would be "clamped". On the operating table, the victim voiced to the doctors "I don't want to" (regarding the procedure). The operation was preformed regardless.

Sources:
(2015) The Saskatoon StarPhoenix
(2015) CBC News

2005: Saskatoon Royal University Hospital

Recovering from the birth of her fourth child in the hopsital, an Algonquin woman was told that because she "was twenty-one and had four kids, that (she) probably shouldn't have any more, that (her) kids would be taken away by Child and Family Services". Additionally, the health staff told her a tubal ligation was contraception.
The woman relunctantly agreed to the surgery before changing her mind. She was told it was too late, that she'd already given her consent.

1977 or 1978: unspecified location

When she was fourteen years old and in her seventh month of pregnancy, an Indigenous woman went to a hospital in Saskatoon after experiencing cramping and spotting. She ended up having a C-section.
What she didn't know, however, was that the surgeon also performed a tubal ligation, removing her left ovary and fallopian tube, without her knowledge or consent.
She didn't find out about it until a visit to a gynecologist decades later, when she was in a relationship and wanted to have children.

1976: Parlimentary inquiry into sterilizations in Nunvaut


A report demanded through parlimentary inquiry revealed that seventy women had been sterilized between 1966-1976 in only six Inuit settlements in part of the Northwest Territories (now Nunvaut). The diagnosis for all but one of these sterilizations was "multiparity" (having given birth multiple times).

Sources:
(2012) Karen Stote

In 1976, a series of articles by Robert Lechat alleged that the federal government was in fact intensifying a program of sterilizing Aboriginal peoples in the North. He claimed that women were sterilized without full knowledge or consent in six communities in the Keewatin District. For example, he alleged that in Repulse Bay, out of twenty-two women between thirty and fifty years of age, ten were sterilized (45 percent).

Sterilizations reports later showed that out of these six settlements, 60 women of childbearing age out of 442 (7.4%) had been sterilized between 1966-1976.

Additional information drawn from this same collection of Medical Services files reveals that the sterilizations reported were only a fraction of those performed on Indigenous peoples. Between 1970-1973, 180 sterilizations were preformed in "Northern Settlements" (no additional information is provided).
